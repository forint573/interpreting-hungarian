# Literary translation into Hungarian (reference)

Read this file **before drafting** whenever the source text is fiction, poetry, drama, song lyrics, creative nonfiction, children's literature, or any text whose voice matters as much as its content. It encodes the norms of the Hungarian **műfordítás** tradition and the transfer operations professional EN→HU translators use. The core grammar diagnostics stay in `../SKILL.md`; this file governs voice, form, and literary convention.

---

## 1. The norm you are translating under

Hungarian has one of Europe's strongest literary translation traditions, and it settled on a clear ideal more than a century ago: **a translation must stand as a Hungarian literary work in its own right** — the reader should never feel a foreign sentence behind the Hungarian one. This is the tradition of Arany János (Hamlet, Szentivánéji álom), the Nyugat generation — Babits Mihály, Kosztolányi Dezső, Tóth Árpád, Szabó Lőrinc — and their successors (Devecseri Gábor, Göncz Árpád, Nádasdy Ádám). Kosztolányi's formula for the discipline this takes: *„műfordítani annyi, mint gúzsba kötötten táncolni"* — translating is dancing while bound hand and foot (Nyugat, 1913).

Two poles of the tradition, both instructive:

- **The Nyugat masters** (Rába György's label: *a szép hűtlenek*, "the beautiful unfaithful") prioritized the poem's beauty and Hungarian voice, even at the cost of small liberties. Lesson: naturalness and effect outrank word-level fidelity.
- **The modern corrective** (Nádasdy Ádám's *A csökkenő költőiség*, 2021; Barna Imre's 2015 *Rozsban a fogó*): do not make the Hungarian *more* poetic, more ornate, or more archaic than the original. Translate what the author wrote, in living contemporary Hungarian, with the register the author chose. Barna Imre on retranslating Salinger: the task is to spread the lie that the author wrote it in Hungarian — and Salinger's material was spoken language, so the Hungarian must breathe spoken Hungarian.

Working defaults that follow:

1. Reproduce the **effect and register**, not the syntax. Restructure freely (see §6).
2. Add no ornament the original lacks; delete no texture it has. Match plainness with plainness.
3. Archaize only if the original is archaic for its own readers — and then use light patina, not Jókai-pastiche.
4. Every character must sound like a person who exists in Hungarian: test dialogue by ear.

## 2. Dialogue: the gondolatjel convention

Hungarian prose fiction marks dialogue with **dashes, not quotation marks** (AkH. 12. §259). Convert English `"…" she said` dialogue to this format in every piece of narrative fiction. The mechanics:

- Each speaker's turn opens a **new paragraph** starting with a gondolatjel + space: `– Szia!`
- A narrator tag after speech is joined by a spaced dash. A sentence-final **period is dropped** before the tag; **`?`, `!` and `…` are kept**. The tag starts lowercase and ends with a period.
- If the same speaker continues with a **new sentence** after the tag, close the tag with a period and reopen with a dash + capital.
- If the tag interrupts **mid-sentence**, close it without a period; the required comma sits **after** the second dash, and the speech resumes lowercase.
- A narrator sentence **before** the speech ends in a colon; the speech starts on the next line with a dash.

Patterns to copy exactly:

> – Szia – mondta Péter. – Gyere be!
>
> – Nem hiszel nekem – állapította meg szomorúan –, pedig igazat mondok.
>
> – Hol voltál? – kérdezte az anyja.
>
> Az asszony halkan megszólalt:
> – Ezt nem kellett volna.

Quotation marks **„…”** stay in use for: quotes inside narration, written matter quoted in the text (letters, signs), titles in sentence-form, and marked inner speech — *„Mennyire hiányzott!” – gondolta.* A quote inside a quote takes **»…«**. Unspoken thought may also run unmarked as free indirect discourse (§4) — in fiction, prefer that or the „…” + tag solution; do not italicize thoughts English-style.

In drama/screenplay format, keep the source's structural layout (NAME: line) and apply everything else in this file.

## 3. Narration and tense

- Hungarian has **one past tense**. The whole English past system (simple, progressive, perfect, past perfect) maps onto it; aspect is carried by coverbs and adverbs, not tense. *He had already left when she arrived.* → **Mire a nő megérkezett, ő már elment.** The archaic narrative past (*mondá, vala*) died out in the 19th century — never use it outside deliberate pastiche.
- **No backshift, ever.** Reported thought and speech keep the tense of the original utterance: *He said he was tired.* → **Azt mondta, hogy fáradt.** (*Azt mondta, hogy fáradt **volt*** means "he said he **had been** tired" — a different claim.) *She knew he would come.* → **Tudta, hogy el fog jönni / eljön.**
- **Historical present** is a live device in Hungarian narration; you may keep an English present-tense narration in present.
- **Free indirect discourse** (szabad függő beszéd): English signals it by backshifted tense + present-time deixis ("Tomorrow was Monday"). Hungarian has no tense signal, so carry the character's voice with lexis, particles, and word order instead: discourse particles (*hát, persze, ugyan, bezzeg, na*), exclamations and questions in narrative past, the character's own vocabulary. *Tomorrow was Monday. Monday, the start of another school week.* → **Holnap hétfő. Hétfő, kezdődik megint az iskola.**

## 4. Verse and song

- The Hungarian norm is **formahű fordítás**: reproduce the original's **meter and rhyme scheme** as well as its meaning. Hungarian verse culture (and reader expectation) treats form as part of the content — Shakespeare stays in iambic pentameter, sonnets stay sonnets, Baudelaire keeps his rhymes.
- The sanctioned relaxation, argued by Nádasdy for his Dante (Magvető, 2016): for **long narrative poems**, keep the meter strictly and drop the rhyme if chasing it would force padding, inversion, or preciosity — *„Tizennégyezer sor nem lehet végig szép"*. For short lyric poems, rhyme loss is a real loss; work for it.
- Priorities when everything cannot be saved: (1) tone and image, (2) meter, (3) rhyme, (4) word-level meaning. Hungarian's stress-initial words and agglutination make rhyme easy but *clean* rhyme (tiszta rím) can sound naive; assonance (asszonánc) is the professional default — Arany's practice.
- **Song lyrics and anything meant to be performed**: singability first — match syllable count and stressed beats to the melody, keep open vowels on held notes, then maximize meaning. A singable 80%-faithful line beats an unsingable literal one.
- Light verse, nonsense, and wordplay-driven verse license full creative rebuilding in the Romhányi tradition (his rhymed *Frédi és Béni* dub is the house classic of gain-in-translation): keep the game, replace the counters.

## 5. Names, titles, realia

**Personal names — the modern norm** (Horváth, Névtani Értesítő 2020): in fiction for adults, **names stay as they are**: Hans Castorp remains Hans Castorp, John Smith remains John Smith (English given-name–first order kept). Do not invert to Hungarian order and do not translate given names — *Verne Gyula* and *May Károly* are fossils of a dead 19th-century practice, kept only for those authors themselves.

Exceptions, in scope order:
- **Children's literature and fantasy Hungarianize speaking names** — creatively, not literally. The canonical models: Karinthy's *Micimackó* (Róbert Gida, Malacka, Füles, Zsebibaba); Réz Ádám / Göncz Árpád's *A Gyűrűk Ura* (Zsákos Frodó, Csavardi Samu, Völgyzugoly); Tóth Tamás Boldizsár's Harry Potter (Roxfort, McGalagony, Perselus Piton, mugli, kviddics — sound-alike coinages that keep the connotation: *galagonya*, *piton*). Translate a speaking name only if its meaning does characterizing work; invent with the same phonetic playfulness as these models.
- **Established Hungarian forms of historical/mythical figures are obligatory**: Kolumbusz Kristóf, Luther Márton, Nagy Sándor, Szent Péter, Odüsszeusz.
- Nicknames and endearments are translated (*Bunny → Nyuszi*), and Hungarian diminutives (-i, -ka/-ke, -csi: Peti, Petike) render English hypocoristics (Tommy, Petey).

**Titles of works**: if a published Hungarian title exists, it is **obligatory** — *A Gyűrűk Ura*, *Rómeó és Júlia*, *Száz év magány*, *Rozsban a fogó* (Salinger since 2015; the 1964 title *Zabhegyező* only when the old translation itself is meant). If no Hungarian title exists, translate it and give the original in parentheses on first mention. Hungarian title capitalization: **first word and proper names only** (*Egri csillagok*, *A kis herceg*); periodicals capitalize content words (*Élet és Tudomány*).

**Realia and measurements** (Tellinger 2007; Klaudy–Simigné 2000):
- In fiction, imperial units are **local color — keep them** in their Hungarian names: *mérföld, láb, hüvelyk, yard, font, gallon*. A Sherlock Holmes who walks kilometers has been damaged.
- Where the reader needs a size, **round inside the prose** (*tíz yardnyira* → *vagy kilenc méterre* only if the scene needs it); never convert with false precision — "about 150 feet" is *vagy ötven méter*, not *45,72 méter*.
- Pragmatic and technical texts (manuals, journalism, nonfiction argument) **do** convert to metric.
- Cultural realia (foods, institutions, school grades): keep + unobtrusive gloss if comprehension demands (*a sheriff helyettese*); domesticate only in children's literature. Decide per item what the scene needs — the strategy question is honosítás (domestication) vs. idegenítés (foreignization), and modern practice is foreignizing for names, pragmatic for measures, domesticating only where the item is incidental.

## 6. The transfer operations (Klaudy's átváltási műveletek)

The standard Hungarian toolbox (Klaudy Kinga: *Bevezetés a fordítás gyakorlatába*; Klaudy–Simigné: *Angol–magyar fordítástechnika*). These are not licenses to paraphrase at will — they are the specific, named moves that turn English structure into Hungarian structure. The ones that matter most EN→HU:

| Operation | What it is | EN → HU |
|---|---|---|
| konkretizálás | specify a general word | *said* → **felelte, dünnyögte, csattant fel** (Hungarian dialogue tags hate repetition of *mondta*) |
| generalizálás | generalize where Hungarian lacks the split | *toes* → **lábujjak**; *brother* → **fivér/öcs/báty** by context |
| felbontás | split a sentence | one 40-word English period → two natural Hungarian sentences |
| összevonás | merge sentences | choppy English staccato → one flowing Hungarian sentence where the rhythm asks |
| betoldás | add what Hungarian grammar/culture needs | *Cambridge* → **a Cambridge-i Egyetem** on first mention if the context doesn't show it's a university |
| kihagyás | drop what Hungarian doesn't need | subject pronouns, possessives, *there is*, most of *his/her* |
| antonim fordítás | negate the opposite | *Keep off the grass* → **Fűre lépni tilos**; *He remembered* → **Nem felejtette el** where idiom prefers it |
| teljes átalakítás | total rebuild of an idiom/cultural item | *It's raining cats and dogs* → **Zuhog, mintha dézsából öntenék** |
| kompenzálás | repay a loss elsewhere | a pun that dies in line 3 is reborn as a pun in line 5; dialect flavor lost on one word returns as colloquial morphology nearby |
| szórendi áthelyezés | word-order rearrangement | English end-focus → Hungarian **pre-verbal focus**; trailing adverbials move forward |
| grammatikai csere | class/voice/structure swap | English passive → 3rd-plural active; noun string → *-i* adjective + noun; adjective → adverb |

Klaudy's empirical law worth internalizing: translators systematically **explicitate** (specify, split, add) more than they implicitate. In literary prose, run the opposite check too — where the original is compact and suggestive, keep it compact; do not explain the metaphor.

## 7. Anti-translationese checklist (fordításnyelv / kvázi-helyesség)

Klaudy's term for the failure mode: **kvázi-helyesség** — every sentence grammatical, the whole text foreign, because source-language structure has shifted the *distribution* of Hungarian patterns. The documented tells of translated Hungarian; sweep the draft for each:

1. **kerül-passive bureaucratese**: *megrendezésre kerül, kiválasztásra került* → **megrendezik, kiválasztották**. Same for empty *történik* and *valósul meg*.
2. **által-agents**: *a bizottság által elfogadott javaslat* is legal register; in prose → **a javaslat, amelyet a bizottság elfogadott**, or recast active.
3. **Pronoun and possessive over-explicitation**: excess *ő, az, ön* and *az én/a te* where the suffix already says it. English *your computer* in UI/prose → **a számítógép(ed)**, not *az ön számítógépe* at every turn.
4. **egy-inflation**: English *a/an* leaking in — *Egy férfi vagyok, aki…* → **Férfi vagyok, aki…**; predicate nouns and generic statements drop *egy*.
5. **amely-stiffness and ami-errors**: *amely* in dialogue sounds starched (use *ami/amit*); after a whole-clause antecedent only **ami** is grammatical (*Elkésett, ami mindenkit bosszantott*).
6. **Superfluous coverbs** copied from phrasal verbs: *feltelepíti a szoftvert* → **telepíti**; *lekommunikál* → **közöl, megbeszél**.
7. **English word-order residue**: subject-first sentences marching in a row, focusless flat intonation, trailing time/place adverbials. Re-run topic–focus assignment (SKILL.md).
8. **-va/-ve overuse for events**: *A macska fel van mászva a fára* is comic; states only (*Az ajtó zárva van*). Sentence-initial participle clauses usually become finite clauses (*Walking home, he…* → **Miközben hazafelé sétált, …**).
9. **Tag monotony**: pages of *mondta* — Hungarian prose varies dialogue tags (konkretizálás) and often drops them once speakers are established.
10. **Untranslated rhythm**: if every Hungarian sentence has the same length as its English source, felbontás/összevonás was never applied. Read the page aloud; Hungarian prose breathes in longer arcs than English journalism, shorter than German officialese.
11. **The missing uniques** (Tirkkonen-Condit's unique-items hypothesis, confirmed for Hungarian on the Pannónia Corpus — *szokott* occurs at half its native rate in translations): the English gave you no cue for *szokott*, *hadd*, *tessék*, *-lak/-lek*, the associative *-ék*, diminutives, or the particles *ugye, hát, ám, bezzeg* — so a translated page tends to have none. Authentic Hungarian prose does. Where dialogue or narration would naturally carry one, put it back.

## 8. Register, slang, dialect

- **Recreate the speech act, not the lexicon.** The Rozsban a fogó lesson: Salinger's teen vernacular must become *living* Hungarian teen vernacular, register-true even where word-untrue. Test: could a Hungarian of that age, class, and decade say this sentence?
- **Slang ages fastest**: prefer durable colloquialism (*tök jó, meló, pasi, csaj*) over this year's meme-slang, unless the original is deliberately ephemeral.
- **English regional dialect** (Cockney, Southern US, Scots) does **not** map onto Hungarian regional dialects — a Texan speaking in palóc reads as parody. Render dialect as social register: colloquial morphology (*-ba/-be* for *-ban/-ben* in eye-dialect, *nem-e tudja*), simplified syntax, characteristic vocabulary; and compensate (§6) where it thins.
- **Profanity**: match force, not anatomy. English *fucking* as an intensifier is usually *kurva* + adjective (*kurva nagy*), not an anatomical calque; Hungarian swearing is rich — use its own idiom at equal strength. Do not bowdlerize and do not escalate.
- **T/V in dialogue**: assign *te/maga/ön* per relationship and keep it consistent per pair and period-appropriate (pre-1945 formality differs from 2020s startup speech). A switch from *magázás* to *tegeződés* between characters is a plot event in Hungarian — place it deliberately (English "call me Jane" moments are the natural anchor).

## 9. Working method for a literary passage

1. Read the whole passage; identify narrator stance, register, rhythm, and any form constraints (verse, refrain, wordplay).
2. Decide the T/V map of every speaking pair and the treatment of names/titles/realia (§5) **before** sentence one.
3. Draft freely under the norm of §1 — restructure with the operations of §6, format dialogue per §2, keep tense discipline per §3.
4. Sweep with the translationese checklist (§7).
5. Read the result aloud as Hungarian literature. Anything you would not find in a Magvető or Európa edition, rewrite.
